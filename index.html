§ 01 The Opening

The leaders we talk to are not behind because they failed. They are behind because the ground shifted.

This is the most uneven adoption curve in the history of enterprise technology. We operate inside it daily, and we will say this plainly: it is hard. It is hard for us, and we are immersed in it.

The organizations we work with span the full distribution. Some have no strategy and know it. Some have a strategy and are six months into executing the wrong one. Some have genuine traction in one function and a vacuum in the other nine. A handful have real maturity, and those leaders are the most worried of all, because they can see how much further there is to go.

What every leader shares, regardless of where they sit on this curve, is a quiet suspicion that the version of AI strategy they are currently pursuing will not be the version that matters in twelve months. They are usually right.

This report is our attempt to share what we see from the operator seat. Not predictions. Not hype. Patterns. The kind of patterns you can only see when you are inside dozens of organizations at once, watching the same mistakes, the same breakthroughs, and the same inflection points repeat across industries and revenue bands.

If you are a leader reading this, our first goal is to make you feel less alone. Our second is to make the moment navigable.

§ 02 In the Field

Five patterns we have observed in the past week alone.

These are not hypothetical. Each describes a conversation we had with a real executive at a real organization in the past seven days. We share them because every leader thinks their situation is unique. It almost never is.

01
Role without roadmap Seen 4× this week

The newly-minted Head of AI who does not know what to do next

Executives we meet have had "AI" added to their title in the last quarter, usually appended to an existing role: Head of Strategy and AI, CTO and AI, COO and AI. The title change is faster than the mandate. They were handed a remit without a roadmap, a budget without a benchmark, and an expectation without a precedent.

What it really means

Boards and CEOs are creating AI ownership before they have a theory of the work. The instinct is correct. The execution is isolating.

Title ≠ mandate
Speed of title change vs. clarity of mandate.
02
Strategy lag Seen 7× this week

The organization still in early-stage strategy, six months after they thought they would be done

We regularly walk into companies still in assessment mode. Committees. Vendor evaluations. Pilot frameworks. Proof-of-concept debates. Meanwhile, individuals in the organization are already using AI every day — often with personal accounts on public tools, often with proprietary company data.

What it really means

The strategy conversation is moving slower than the usage reality. That gap is the single biggest governance risk most organizations have today and cannot see.

Two speeds
Leadership strategy vs. workforce usage, same clock.
03
Adoption chasm Visible in every org

Adoption that swings from the lowest floor to the highest ceiling inside the same company

One team is ten times more productive than the team next to them. Marketing is shipping work in hours that used to take weeks. Finance is still waiting for a policy. The super-user gap is not abstract. It is measurable, visible, and it shows up as promotion decisions, compensation debates, and talent departures.

What it really means

AI is no longer a tools conversation. It is a workforce restructuring event — and most organizations do not yet have the language for it.

Productivity divergence
Same company. Same quarter. Different universes.
04
The SaaS trap Dominant mistake

Six months spent choosing an LLM provider as if it were a SaaS purchase

We have watched leadership teams run formal evaluations to pick "the one." Procurement playbooks. Feature matrices. Vendor scorecards. The assumption underneath is that the choice is durable, the way a CRM or ERP would be durable. It is not. Capabilities converge quarterly. Practitioners ahead of this curve are not choosing a provider — they are building a coordination layer that can switch providers, combine them, and route work intelligently between them.

What it really means

Companies treating this as a SaaS decision will re-run the decision in eighteen months with the scars of a switching cost they did not anticipate.

Mental model shift
From vendor selection to orchestration architecture.
05
Cognitive load Universal

Leaders who are exhausted and do not want to admit it

Every week we hear a version of: "I do not know where to go. I do not know who to trust. I do not know how to tell the signal from the noise." The tone is usually apologetic, as if it is a personal failing. It is not. The volume of change is genuinely difficult to metabolize, even for full-time practitioners. The executives we respect most are willing to say it out loud.

What it really means

The market is confused. If leaders feel frustrated now, the next ninety days will be worse, not better. That is not a reason to panic. It is a reason to stop trying to navigate this alone.

Rate of change
Volume of capabilities, pricing, and regulation to metabolize.

§ 04 What Is Coming

Four shifts already visible from the operator seat.

Not forecasts drawn from conference keynotes. Trajectories already moving through vendor roadmaps, procurement conversations, and the week-over-week work we see inside organizations ahead of the curve.

01

Pricing inverts. Economics move on-premise.

The signal flare went up mid-April when Anthropic moved Claude Enterprise from flat-rate subscriptions to a base-seat-plus-usage model — heavy users projected to pay 2×–3× more at renewal. This is not an isolated vendor decision. It is the beginning of a structural shift driven by the underlying economics of compute.

What leaders will face

TCO becomes a CFO-level conversation. Expect serious evaluation of hybrid architectures, local model deployment, and private inference. Organizations that plan this in 2026 will pay a fraction of what reactive organizations pay in 2027.

Fig. 4.1 · Pricing inversion
Per-seat cost under flat-rate (old) vs. seat-plus-usage (new).
02

Governance frameworks that do not exist yet become audit requirements.

The SEC has named AI and cybersecurity as its top 2026 examination priorities, displacing crypto. The EU AI Act is live. U.S. federal posture is diverging from European, creating a compliance matrix most organizations are not equipped to manage. Stanford recorded 233 harmful AI-related incidents in 2024, a 56% year-over-year increase.

What leaders will face

Regulators have stopped accepting "innovation" as a defense. The cheapest time to build AI governance is before you are required to have it. Most organizations will do this under pressure — the most expensive path available.

Fig. 4.2 · Governance risk matrix
Most organizations plot in the high-pressure, low-readiness quadrant.
03

The coordination layer becomes the competitive moat.

The question stops being "which LLM are we on" and becomes "how do our agents coordinate across CRM, finance, marketing, and operations." Gartner projects 15% of daily work decisions will be made autonomously by agentic AI by 2028, up from effectively zero today. The orchestration layer becomes as strategically important as the agents themselves.

What leaders will face

Organizations that architect for multi-agent, multi-model, multi-function coordination now will compound advantages that retrofitting cannot replicate.

Fig. 4.3 · Agentic work projection
Autonomous share of daily work decisions, 2026 → 2028.
04

Moral drift enters the boardroom vocabulary.

Model drift is the technical term. Moral drift is the business term. It describes what happens when AI systems begin producing outputs that no longer align with your company's values, brand voice, legal obligations, or customer commitments — often invisibly, often only recognized after a customer notices first. Corporate risk heat maps are already adding Ethical Alignment as a standalone category.

What leaders will face

The next generation of enterprise risk management includes continuous observability into AI behavior. Organizations that build this proactively avoid the incidents that will define cautionary tales for the rest of the decade.

§ 05 The Honest Part

Nobody is keeping up. Not the leaders. Not the analysts. Not us.

The feeling of being behind is not a signal that you are bad at your job. It is a signal that you are paying attention.

We run two firms whose entire reason for existing is to stay at the frontier of applied AI. We have dashboards, daily briefings, private networks, model access, and hands-on practitioner work across dozens of organizations. We still find ourselves, every week, learning about a capability, a pricing change, a regulatory development, or a field pattern that reshapes how we think about the work.

If it is hard for us, it is more than fair that it is hard for the leaders we work with — the ones with a business to run, a P&L to defend, a board to answer to, and a workforce to guide.

The leaders who feel most confident right now are usually the least informed. The leaders who feel most uncertain are usually the ones thinking most clearly about the real shape of the problem. The answer is not to work harder at keeping up. It is to restructure how your organization metabolizes change — a different problem, and the one we exist to solve.

§ 06 · What to take away

The work of this moment is not picking the right tool. It is building the operating capacity to keep making good decisions while the tools change underneath you.

The organizations strongest at the end of this decade will not be the ones that chose the best LLM in 2026. They will be the ones that built the internal muscle to evaluate, integrate, govern, and redirect AI continuously as the landscape moves.

That muscle does not come from a software purchase. It comes from how you structure ownership, how you build feedback loops, how you make governance a design principle rather than a post-incident response, and how you stop trying to solve this as a one-time project.

An invitation

If any of this resonates with where your organization sits right now, we would welcome a conversation. Not to sell you anything. To compare notes. The only way any of us navigates a moment like this is by sharing what we are seeing, honestly, with people who are paying attention.
